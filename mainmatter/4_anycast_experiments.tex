\chapter{International Data Transfers in Anycast Ecosystems}
\label{ch:anycast_experiments}

The previous chapter introduced Hunter, a methodology capable of inferring the geographic destinations of anycast communications through active network measurements and latency-based multilateration techniques. While the validation results demonstrated the feasibility and reliability of the proposed approach, the actual impact of anycast infrastructures on data protection and data sovereignty remains an open question. In particular, it is necessary to understand whether modern Internet services effectively produce international transfers of personal data that remain unknown to users, developers, and regulatory transparency mechanisms.

Mobile ecosystems constitute a particularly suitable environment for analyzing this phenomenon due to their global scale, the central role they play in users' daily activities, and their extensive dependence on external network services. Modern smartphones continuously interact with remote infrastructures to provide functionalities such as content synchronization, analytics, advertising, authentication, notifications, or multimedia delivery. As a consequence, mobile devices generate a large volume of network communications involving multiple service providers and jurisdictions.

The Android ecosystem is especially appropriate for this analysis because of its worldwide adoption, the openness of its application distribution model, and the extensive use of Third-Party Libraries (TPLs). Android applications rarely operate as isolated software components and instead integrate external SDKs and services responsible for analytics, advertising, crash reporting, authentication, content delivery, or user tracking functionalities. These libraries could establish communications independently from the application developer, frequently without explicit visibility regarding the final infrastructure receiving the transmitted data.

From a regulatory perspective, this lack of geographic transparency introduces substantial challenges. Modern privacy regulations such as the GDPR \cite{REGULATIONSRelevance} impose strict obligations regarding international transfers of personal data, including transparency requirements concerning the jurisdictions where data may be processed or accessed. However, anycast infrastructures fundamentally decouple logical service identifiers from physical geographic destinations. Consequently, the jurisdictions effectively reached by Internet communications may differ from those declared in privacy policies or assumed by service providers.

This chapter presents a large-scale empirical analysis of international data transfers performed through anycast infrastructures within the Android ecosystem. Using Hunter as the core geographic inference mechanism, the study analyzes the destinations reached by communications generated by Android applications and their embedded TPLs. The objective is twofold. First, the chapter seeks to quantify the prevalence of international data transfers associated with anycast communications and characterize their geographic behavior. Second, it evaluates whether the observed destinations align with the transparency obligations established by current data protection frameworks.

The analysis is structured progressively. First, the experimental methodology used for traffic collection, privacy policy analysis, and geographic inference is presented. Next, the chapter analyzes the existence of cross-border communications observed in Android applications, including their temporal variability and the prevalence of destinations located outside the EEA. Afterwards, the observed destinations are compared against the jurisdictions declared in privacy policies in order to evaluate transparency and disclosure compliance. The role of TPLs as one of the main drivers of these communications is then analyzed in detail. Finally, the chapter extends the discussion beyond the Android ecosystem and presents a broader perspective on jurisdictional exposure in operational anycast infrastructures, motivating the need for deterministic geographic control mechanisms discussed in subsequent chapters.

\section{Experimental Methodology}
\label{sc:anycast_experiments_methodology}

This chapter presents a large-scale empirical analysis of international data transfers performed through anycast infrastructures in the Android ecosystem. The objective of the experimental methodology is to capture the network communications generated by Android applications and their embedded Third-Party Libraries (TPLs), infer the geographic destinations reached by these communications using Hunter, and compare the observed jurisdictions with the information disclosed in the corresponding privacy policies.

The methodology combines dynamic traffic analysis, active network measurements, and automatic inspection of privacy policies in order to characterize both the technical and regulatory dimensions of the problem. \subsection{Android Application Dataset}
\label{sc:anycast_experiments_dataset}

The experiments were conducted using a large-scale dataset of Android applications obtained from the Google Play Store. The dataset and part of the traffic collection infrastructure were built upon previous work focused on privacy analysis in mobile ecosystems and third-party tracking behaviors.

In total, the analysis considered 5,759 Android applications spanning multiple categories of the Google Play ecosystem, including communication, entertainment, productivity, finance, gaming, shopping, and social networking applications \cite{AnycastTPLPaperReference}. The objective of this selection was to obtain a representative view of widely deployed Android applications actively used by end users and integrating modern cloud-based services and Third-Party Libraries (TPLs).

The analyzed applications exhibit a strong dependence on external infrastructures and embedded SDKs responsible for functionalities such as analytics, advertising, authentication, crash reporting, content delivery, and user tracking. This characteristic makes the Android ecosystem particularly suitable for studying the impact of anycast communications on international personal data transfers.

In addition to the applications themselves, the experiments also analyzed the TPL ecosystem embedded within the collected applications. Since Android applications frequently delegate substantial functionality to external libraries, analyzing only the behavior of the main application package would provide an incomplete representation of the generated communications and associated privacy implications.

\subsection{Traffic Collection and Analysis}
\label{sc:anycast_experiments_traffic_collection}

The traffic analysis methodology was partially based on a previously developed mobile privacy analysis framework designed to monitor the communications established by Android applications and embedded Third-Party Libraries during execution.

The applications were executed in controlled Android environments where their network traffic could be intercepted and inspected. During execution, the applications were manually interacted with in order to trigger realistic communication patterns and activate the functionalities commonly used by end users.

HTTPS communications were inspected through a TLS interception proxy that enabled the extraction of destination domains, remote IP addresses, HTTP metadata, and additional network parameters required for the geographic inference analysis. The collected traffic traces allowed identifying both first-party communications generated directly by the applications and third-party communications established by embedded TPLs.

From the collected traffic traces, more than 4.2 million network connections were analyzed, leading to the identification of approximately 200 anycast IP addresses associated with operational Internet services and third-party infrastructures \cite{AnycastTPLPaperReference}.

\subsection{Privacy Policy Analysis}
\label{sc:anycast_experiments_privacy_policies}

To evaluate the transparency implications of the observed communications, the privacy policies associated with the analyzed applications and TPLs were manually collected and reviewed.

The analysis focused on identifying explicit references to international data transfers, third-party data sharing practices, and the jurisdictions where personal data could potentially be processed or accessed. Particular attention was devoted to determining whether the countries inferred through Hunter were explicitly disclosed in the corresponding privacy documentation.

The privacy policy analysis revealed a highly heterogeneous ecosystem. While some applications explicitly listed the countries where personal data may be processed, many others only provided generic statements regarding international transfers or simply referenced the use of external service providers without identifying the associated jurisdictions.

A similar situation was observed for TPLs. Many embedded libraries either lacked publicly accessible privacy policies or provided only broad descriptions of their data processing activities without specifying the countries involved in the observed communications.

The comparison between declared and observed jurisdictions enabled evaluating the degree of transparency effectively provided to end users regarding international transfers performed through anycast infrastructures.

\subsection{Anycast Destination Inference}
\label{sc:anycast_experiments_hunter_integration}

The geographic destinations associated with the captured communications were inferred using Hunter, the methodology presented in Chapter~\ref{ch:hunter}.

For each communication involving a candidate anycast destination, Hunter executed distributed network measurements in order to estimate the country where the serving anycast replica was physically deployed. The methodology combined traceroute analysis, last-hop identification, latency-based multilateration, and country inference techniques to estimate the jurisdictions effectively reached by the communications.

The inference process was periodically repeated for selected destinations in order to capture the dynamic behavior of operational anycast infrastructures and identify potential temporal variations in the geographic destinations reached by the communications.

The results produced by Hunter were subsequently integrated with the traffic analysis and privacy policy datasets, enabling the correlation of Android applications, embedded TPLs, observed communications, inferred geographic destinations, and declared jurisdictions within a unified analysis framework.

This integration constitutes the basis for the empirical analysis presented in the following sections, where the prevalence of international transfers, the role of TPLs, and the transparency limitations of current privacy disclosure mechanisms are systematically evaluated.
